How climate change is supercharging wildfires around the world

According to an analysis released in 2020, climate change made the conditions for Australia’s unprecedented 2019-20 bushfires at least 30% more likely. seen anything like it.

Globally, climate change is providing the perfect conditions for wildfires to flourish and consume, consuming larger amounts of land and having massive impacts on humans, wildlife, and the economy.

Fires in the Amazon have reached unusual severity and new highs. Brazils Pantanal, the world’s largest wetland, had reached their highest level since records began in 1998. In addition, the US has entered a record-breaking fire season, projecting more land to be burned by fire than any year previously recorded.

The US fires in 2020 have killed 30 people. Australian fires have killed 9. Billions of dollars in damages and recovery costs are being allocated to combat the rising threat of wildfires, and it’s only set to get worse.

California

Section 1

In California, bushfires have become increasingly ferocious, pushing habitats to the brink and forcing families to abandon their livelihoods and homes. In 2020 alone, more than 4% of the state’s land had burnt. This accounts to 1,779,730 hectares of land with roughly 9,917 separate fires recorded, making 2020 the largest wildfire season recorded in California’s modern history.

There is no indication that these extreme wildfires will start to diminish in their severity or frequency, but in fact, quite the opposite. With the escalation of global warming it’s expected that such extreme natural disasters will become ever more popular and continue to destroy habitats and livelihoods.

Extreme wildfires such as the one seen in California in 2020 also means that the region will continuously see abnormal levels of smoke lingering for months. This has a disastrous impact on the most vulnerable people such as those with asthma, and further, the general population, where air quality dramatically spikes up to 10x that of which is considered “healthy air quality” according to the Air Quality Index (AQI). In 2020 California experienced “Hazardous” air quality for weeks on end. In this category its recommended to “avoid all physical activity outdoors” with an extra warning for “sensitive groups”.

The impacts of California’s wildfires have been disastrous in many aspects of its society. From the loss of habitats, economic impacts and general health complications that are directly correlated with wildfires, California is centre stage experiencing the impacts of a warming climate.

Australia

Section 2

2019 was host to some of the most disastrous bushfires Australia has ever seen. The environmental, economic, and personal damages experienced during this period can be hard to comprehend. The 2019-2020 Australian bushfire season consumed approximately 18,636,079 hectares of land. That’s over 10x the land burned during 2020 in California and comparable to the size of the United Kingdom. The fires were felt right across the country, hundreds of homes were lost, billions of animals killed, and a toxic air quality was felt throughout regional areas and big cities. This hazardous smoke pollution was further felt by Australia’s neighbouring country New Zealand.

Nearly 3 billion animals consisting of mammals, reptiles, birds, and frogs were killed by Australia’s devastating 2019-2020 bushfires. This figure is broken down into 143 million mammals, 2.46 billion reptiles, 180 million birds and 51 million frogs.

Guardian Australia has spoken to some of the millions of people touched by Australia's summer of dread. Here, we take you on a journey through some of the country's worst-affected areas.

Australia has been getting consistently hotter, recording new temperature records for the past 4 decades. The country broke its all-time high temperature record twice in December. An average maximum temperature of 40.9C was recorded on 17 December, broken a day later by 41.9C. This follows 4 other all time high records dating back from 1980.

Amazon Rainforest

Section 3

In 2020, more than 2,500 major fires burned across the Brazilian Amazon, burning over 906,000 hectares of land. The uniqueness of these fires stem from the location in which they burn. Wildfires are in fact quite rare in tropical rainforests due the high humidity and wet conditions. The flora is commonly too wet for lightning to spark a fire or for accidental fires to gain much ground. But nevertheless, we have seen the largest rainforest in the world burn vigorously.

The Amazon is home to the most biodiverse species on Earth. The fires witnessed throughout 2020 have radically damaged this ecosystem by polluting rivers and lands, diminishing food sources, degrading the soil, and causing large quantities of land to dry out. The economic fallout is estimated to cost Brazil anywhere from $957 billion to $3.5 trillion US dollars over a 30-year period.

Some scientists fear that a combination of fires, increasing drought due to climate change, and deforestation could lead to a tipping point for the Amazon which harbours roughly 10% of the world’s biodiversity.

Since forest fires are a newer phenomenon in the Amazon, the time in which the rainforest recovers after such devastating events is not known or if a recovery occurs in the first place. The Amazons dry seasons are getting longer, and mega-droughts are becoming more common, driven by climate change and deforestation.

The Cost

Section 4

The damages inflicted by these catastrophic bushfires across the world are immense. We know that mass amounts of ecosystems are burnt along with billions of animals and habitats. But this extends much further. In the 2019-2020 Australian bushfires the economic costs have been some of the highest the country has ever seen in any natural disaster before it.

An unprecedented $100 billion dollars have been set aside as a concequence of the black summer bushfires, including 2,448 homes destroyed and 26 lives lost according to the Australian Institute for Disaster Resilience. The black summer also accounted for approximately 61,000 koala deaths, a species listed as critically endangered.

Are we there yet?

Section 5

During the 2019 Australian bushfires a staggering number of habitats and animals were burnt down and or killed. The devastating bushfires have consumed nearly 3 billion animals according to leading scientists who have revealed the scale of the impact on native wildlife throughout the country.

According to The Guardian an estimated 143 million mammals, 180 million birds, 51 million frogs and an extraordinary 2.5 billion reptiles were severely affected by the fires that occurred in Australia during the end of 2019.

It’s clear that the world will continue to see unprecedented natural events such as the extremely damaging wildfires we’ve witnessed across the world. As a consequence of global warming the world may further see the rapid deterioration of habitats, species and livelihoods.
